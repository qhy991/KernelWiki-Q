## Challenge

The split_kv heuristic computes an uncapped split count (split_wave_aware) that can exceed sm_count/2, launching too many sequential split kernels (16 in this scenario) that each perform atomicAdd into a shared output buffer, creating potential for excessive atomic contention under real FMHA workloads


## Solution

Cap split_kv at sm_count/2 when split_wave_aware exceeds 1 and fused reduction is active, reducing the number of concurrent split kernels from 16 to 8 and limiting atomic accumulation pressure on the shared output buffer

## Key Lessons

- Cap split_kv at sm_count/2 when fused reduction is active and split_wave_aware > 1 — this limits concurrent split kernels to at most half the SMs, reducing atomic contention on the shared accumulation buffer in real FMHA workloads with heavy shared-memory atomics and TMA loads
- Microbenchmarks with light atomicAdd workloads may not reproduce the contention that the cap is designed to address — the benefit appears only under realistic FMHA pressure from shared-memory atomics and TMA loads
- Heuristic caps on split counts are defensive optimizations: they trade peak parallelism for bounded resource contention, yielding neutral or slight gains in benchmarks but preventing pathological slowdowns in production workloads
