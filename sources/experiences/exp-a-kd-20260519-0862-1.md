## Challenge

The two-category LRU write-back lumps cache misses together with stale evictables at the LRU front, incorrectly treating recently-accessed miss entries as least-recently-used and breaking the LRU eviction policy in multi-request cache behavior


## Solution

Split the two-category write-back into three categories — misses placed just before hits near MRU end, stale evictables at LRU front, hits remain — so that stale evictables are correctly evicted first while miss entries retain higher recency in the LRU order

## Key Lessons

- LRU cache write-back must separate misses from stale evictables into distinct categories so that stale entries occupy the true LRU front — lumping them together breaks eviction priority and causes incorrect cache behavior under multi-request workloads
- Launch-overhead-dominated microbenchmarks (2-3 us kernels) exhibit measurement noise that can produce apparent 10% regressions or improvements unrelated to actual kernel computation changes; correctness fixes should be validated functionally, not by microbenchmark timing
- Adding a third branch for a small category (16 elements < 32-lane warp) introduces negligible warp divergence — the performance impact of adding correctly-ordered branches is far smaller than the correctness cost of using an insufficient two-category scheme
