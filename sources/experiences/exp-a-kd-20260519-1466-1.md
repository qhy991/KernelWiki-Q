## Challenge

GEMM with TileShape<128,128,64> and WarpShape<64,64,64> forces 4 warps to cover a 128-element M tile when actual M is only 8, resulting in only 12.5% valid data per warp with the rest being padding zeroes guarded by predicates — massive warp-level compute waste


## Solution

Replace TileShape<128,128,64>/WarpShape<64,64,64> with TileShape<16,64,128>/WarpShape<16,64,64>, matching the tile M dimension (16) to the problem M (8) for 50% warp utilization instead of 12.5%, while deepening K from 64 to 128 to compensate for reduced M-side parallelism

## Key Lessons

- Match CUTLASS TileShape M dimension to the actual problem M to avoid wasted warp-level compute on out-of-bounds padding — when M is small (e.g., 8), a tile M of 128 forces warps to mostly compute on zeroes
- Compensate for a reduced tile M by deepening tile K (64→128) to maintain pipeline depth and effective data throughput when shrinking the M dimension of the tile
- WarpShape M should not exceed the problem M by a large factor — each warp processes WarpShape-M elements, and elements beyond the valid range are predicated out, wasting ALU cycles and registers
